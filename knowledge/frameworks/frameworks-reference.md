# Atlas Frameworks Reference
**Version 1.0 / 2026.03.31**

This document is a synthesized reference of operative strategic frameworks. It is not a summary of source books — it is the frameworks extracted, made promptable, and organized for use. Each section contains the core model, how to apply it, and where it breaks down.

---

## 1. GOOD STRATEGY / BAD STRATEGY — Richard Rumelt

### The Kernel
Good strategy has three parts that must work together:

**Diagnosis** — A clear-eyed definition of the challenge. Not a goal, not a vision — a frank characterization of the situation that simplifies the complexity and identifies what obstacles are actually in play. Most organizations skip this step and go straight to objectives.

**Guiding Policy** — The approach chosen to deal with the diagnosed challenge. It channels action in certain directions without defining exactly what shall be done. It rules out a range of actions and focuses resources and attention.

**Coherent Actions** — The coordinated set of steps, resource commitments, and practical moves that carry out the guiding policy. Each action reinforces the others. Incoherent actions — each individually reasonable but pulling in different directions — are a hallmark of bad strategy.

### Bad Strategy
Bad strategy is not the absence of strategy. It has four hallmarks:
1. **Fluff** — vague, high-sounding language that creates the appearance of thought without the thought
2. **Failure to face the challenge** — avoiding the hard diagnosis by setting goals instead of addressing obstacles
3. **Mistaking goals for strategy** — "We will become number one" is a goal, not a strategy
4. **Bad strategic objectives** — objectives that ignore critical obstacles or are impractical

### Proximate Objectives
A proximate objective is one that is close enough to be achievable — it can actually be accomplished with the resources and capabilities at hand. Good strategy identifies the proximate objective: the achievable near-term position that unlocks the path toward the longer-term goal.

### When to apply
- Opening a new engagement — use the kernel as a diagnostic scaffold
- Evaluating a client's existing strategy — test for fluff, incoherence, goal-masquerading-as-strategy
- Writing a strategic brief — diagnosis → guiding policy → coherent actions is the architecture

### Where it breaks down
Rumelt assumes a single strategic challenge can be isolated. In complex organizations with multiple competing priorities, the diagnosis becomes genuinely hard — not because people are unclear, but because the challenge is legitimately plural. The kernel is less useful when the real problem is organizational rather than strategic.

## 2. PLAYING TO WIN — Lafley & Martin

### The Strategy Cascade
Strategy is an integrated set of choices. The cascade has five levels, each informing the ones below:

1. **Winning Aspiration** — The purpose of the enterprise. What does winning look like? Not a financial target — a statement of what kind of company this is and what it sets out to achieve in the world.

2. **Where to Play** — The playing field choices: which markets, which customer segments, which channels, which geographies, which product categories. Strategy requires explicit choices about where *not* to play.

3. **How to Win** — The competitive advantage that enables winning on the chosen playing field. Two generic options: cost leadership (better economics than competitors) or differentiation (offering something competitors cannot match at a price customers will pay.

4. **Core Capabilities** — The activities and competencies that must be in place to win where you've chosen to play. What does the organization need to be genuinely excellent at?

5. **Management Systems** — The systems, structures, and measures that support the capabilities. Strategy fails when the management systems don't reinforce the choices made above.

### The Cascade Logic
Each level must be consistent with the others. A differentiation strategy (How to Win) requires differentiated capabilities (Core Capabilities) and management systems that measure and reward differentiation — not cost efficiency. Misalignment between levels is one of the most common causes of strategy failure.

### Reverse Engineering
The cascade can be run forward (vision down to management systems) or backward (start with what you're currently good at and derive where to play). Both are valid. The diagnostic question is: are the five levels coherent?

### When to apply
- Brand and business strategy engagements where the question is competitive positioning
- New business pitches where you're defining the client's strategic logic
- Internal strategy work — where is the incoherence in the cascade?

### Where it breaks down
Playing to Win assumes a relatively stable competitive environment where choices hold for meaningful periods. In rapidly shifting markets (AI-era technology, platform economies), where-to-play and how-to-win can shift faster than the cascade can be written. The framework is better for established categories than for genuinely emergent ones.

## 3. OBVIOUSLY AWESOME — April Dunford

### Positioning as Context-Setting
Positioning is not a tagline. It is the context in which customers understand your product. Get the context wrong and even genuinely good products fail — because customers evaluate them against the wrong competitive alternatives.

### The Five Components of Positioning

1. **Competitive Alternatives** — What would customers do if your product didn't exist? This is the real competition — not the companies you consider competitors, but the actual alternatives customers reach for. Often includes: doing it manually, using a spreadsheet, doing nothing, hiring someone.

2. **Unique Attributes** — The features or capabilities you have that the competitive alternatives do not. Not a feature list — only the attributes that are genuinely differentiated relative to what customers would otherwise use.

3. **Value** — The value those unique attributes enable for customers. Move from attribute to value: the attribute is *what you have*, the value is *what it does for the customer*. Attributes without value translation are a positioning failure.

4. **Target Customer Profile** — The specific customers for whom your value is most compelling. Positioning only works when aimed. The best-fit customer is the one for whom your unique attributes solve a genuinely important problem better than any alternative. Start with them, not with the total addressable market.

5. **Market Category** — The frame of reference in which you are placing the product. Market category immediately sets customer expectations about what you are, who you compete with, and what you cost. Choosing the wrong category is one of the most common and most damaging positioning errors.

### Positioning Traps
- **Positioning to everyone** — dilutes the signal for best-fit customers
- **Leading with attributes** — customers don't buy attributes, they buy outcomes
- **Category default** — accepting an obvious category without evaluating whether a different frame produces more favorable competitive comparisons

### When to apply
- Any brand or product positioning engagement
- New market entry strategy
- Repositioning work — use the five components as diagnostic questions
- Competitive intelligence — reverse-engineer a competitor's positioning

### Where it breaks down
Dunford's framework is product-centric. It works best for companies with a clear, discrete product. For service businesses, consulting practices, or brands with complex portfolios, the "unique attributes" step becomes harder — services are more relationship-dependent than attribute-dependent. Adapt accordingly.

## 4. HOW BRANDS GROW — Byron Sharp

### Mental and Physical Availability
Sharp's central argument: brands grow by increasing mental availability (being thought of in buying situations) and physical availability (being easy to find and buy). Loyalty is largely a byproduct of penetration, not its prerequisite.

**Mental Availability** — The probability that a buyer will notice, think of, or recall a brand in buying situations. Built through consistent, broad reach over time. Mental availability is not brand love — it is cognitive accessibility at the moment of purchase.

**Physical Availability** — Being present in the channels, formats, and moments where buyers are purchasing. Distribution, shelf presence, search visibility, channel breadth.

### Distinctive Brand Assets
The mechanisms through which mental availability is built. A distinctive asset is a brand element — a color, a shape, a character, a sonic identity, a tagline — that is strongly associated with the brand in memory. Distinctive assets must be:
- **Distinctive** — owned by the brand, not generic to the category
- **Consistent** — used repeatedly over time without constant reinvention
- **Integrated** — deployed across touchpoints to reinforce the same memory structure

The test: can someone encounter the asset without the brand name and still know whose it is?

### The Double Jeopardy Law
Smaller brands suffer on two dimensions: they have fewer buyers AND those buyers buy them less often. This is structural, not a failure of loyalty strategy. The implication: growth comes from recruiting new buyers (penetration), not from extracting more from existing ones (loyalty). Both matter, but penetration drives growth.

### Category Entry Points
Buyers don't think about brands constantly. They think about them at moments of need — category entry points. Mental availability means being linked in memory to the relevant entry points: the situations, needs, and contexts that trigger category consideration. Strategy should identify the category entry points that matter most and ensure the brand is linked to them.

### When to apply
- Brand strategy where the question is growth — use as a check against loyalty-first assumptions
- Creative strategy — test whether work builds distinctive assets or undermines them
- Media and channel strategy — physical and mental availability have direct channel implications

### Where it breaks down
Sharp's work is based on established FMCG categories with high purchase frequency. The laws of marketing he describes are empirical patterns — they may hold less strictly in B2B, luxury, or low-frequency purchase categories where relationship and loyalty dynamics are genuinely different. Apply with calibration in those contexts.

## 5. THINKING FAST AND SLOW — Daniel Kahneman

### System 1 and System 2
**System 1** — Fast, automatic, intuitive, associative. Runs constantly, generates impressions and feelings that System 2 endorses or overrides. Does not know what it doesn't know.

**System 2** — Slow, effortful, deliberate, logical. Engaged for complex reasoning but is lazy — it accepts System 1's output unless there is a reason to question it.

Most decisions are made by System 1. Most strategic errors are System 1 errors that System 2 failed to catch.

### Key Biases for Strategic Work

**Anchoring** — The first number or framing encountered disproportionately influences subsequent estimates and decisions. In client work: the initial brief anchors the problem definition, often inappropriately.

**Availability Heuristic** — Probability estimates are distorted by how easily examples come to mind. Recent, vivid, or emotionally resonant events are overweighted. In strategy: the last competitor move gets more weight than the competitive pattern.

**Confirmation Bias** — System 1 finds evidence that confirms existing beliefs and dismisses disconfirming evidence. In diagnosis: actively seeking the evidence that would disprove the hypothesis is a deliberate System 2 intervention.

**Planning Fallacy** — Systematic underestimation of time, cost, and difficulty of future plans, combined with overestimation of benefits. Present bias amplifies it: the costs of the plan feel smaller than they will actually be.

**Halo Effect** — Overall impressions of a person, brand, or company influence judgments about specific attributes. A strong brand gets attributed with strong products even without evidence.

**Loss Aversion** — Losses loom approximately twice as large as equivalent gains. Strategically: the pain of losing share is felt more acutely than the pleasure of gaining it, creating defensive rather than offensive strategic instincts.

**WYSIATI (What You See Is All There Is)** — System 1 builds the most coherent story it can from available information, without accounting for what it doesn't know. Overconfident conclusions are often WYSIATI errors.

### Implications for Diagnosis
DIAGNOSIS mode is a System 2 intervention. It exists to slow down the rush to conclusion, surface evidence that System 1 would dismiss, and name what is unknown. The foundational beliefs of Atlas — "truth before opinion," "sycophancy is a system failure" — are anti-System-1 commitments.

### When to apply
- Use as a checklist during diagnostic work: which biases are most likely active in this situation?
- In PRESCRIPTION mode: name the biases the argument is most vulnerable to
- Presenting to clients: design for System 1 first (what is the immediate impression?), System 2 second (does the argument hold?)

## 6. JOBS TO BE DONE — Clayton Christensen / Tony Ulwick

### The Core Insight
People don't buy products. They hire products to do a job. The job is the progress the customer is trying to make in a given situation. Understanding the job — not the product, not the customer demographic — is the unit of analysis for innovation strategy.

### Three Job Types
**Functional jobs** — The practical task the customer is trying to accomplish: "get from point A to point B," "track my expenses," "communicate a brand position."

**Emotional jobs** — How the customer wants to feel during and after accomplishing the functional job: confident, in control, respected, not anxious.

**Social jobs** — How the customer wants to be perceived by others: competent, innovative, sophisticated, responsible.

Most products address the functional job. The emotional and social jobs are where differentiation often lives.

### Outcome-Driven Innovation (Ulwick)
Ulwick operationalizes JTBD for strategy. Customers evaluate products against desired outcomes — statements of the form: "minimize the time it takes to X," "increase the likelihood that Y." There are typically 50–150 desired outcomes per job. Outcomes are stable even as products and technologies change.

**The Opportunity Algorithm:**
```
Opportunity Score = Importance + max(Importance − Satisfaction, 0)
```
Outcomes that are important but poorly satisfied are the highest-opportunity spaces. Outcomes that are important and well-satisfied are table stakes. Outcomes that are unimportant can be ignored.

### Job Mapping
A job can be mapped as a sequence of steps the customer goes through to complete it. Each step has associated desired outcomes. Job mapping surfaces where in the process customers struggle most — which is often not where products currently focus.

### When to apply
- Innovation and product strategy — use job mapping to find unmet needs
- Positioning — customers positioned by job-to-be-done rather than demographic
- Competitive intelligence — what job is the competitor actually solving?

### Where it breaks down
JTBD is most powerful for functional products with clear use cases. For cultural products — fashion, entertainment, social media — where the job is partially constituted by the product itself, the framework is harder to apply. The "job" exists partly because the product made it conceivable.

## 7. INSPIRED — Marty Cagan

### Discovery vs. Delivery
The fundamental distinction: **discovery** is figuring out what to build (is this valuable, usable, feasible, viable?); **delivery** is building it. Most product teams spend the majority of their time on delivery — executing against a roadmap of requirements. This is the wrong ratio.

Discovery requires testing four risks before building:
1. **Value risk** — Will customers buy or choose to use it?
2. **Usability risk** — Can users figure out how to use it?
3. **Feasibility risk** — Can we build it with our current technology and team?
4. **Business viability risk** — Does it work for our business (legal, financial, marketing, sales)?

### Product-Market Fit
The moment when a product genuinely satisfies a market need at scale. Signs of PMF: customers are upset when they can't use the product, organic growth through word of mouth, retention curves flatten rather than declining to zero. PMF is not declared — it is observed.

The common failure: scaling before achieving PMF. Growth investment amplifies whatever is already present — if the product doesn't have PMF, scaling makes the absence more expensive.

### Opportunity Assessment
Before committing to building, Cagan's opportunity assessment asks:
1. What value does this create for customers?
2. For which customer, exactly?
3. What is the business case?
4. What is the minimum solution required to deliver the value?

The fourth question is strategic: the minimum solution is not a compromise — it is a discipline that forces clarity about what the actual value is.

### Continuous Discovery
Cagan's modern emphasis: product discovery is not a phase — it is a continuous activity. Weekly customer interviews, rapid prototyping, and constant testing are operating conditions, not project phases.

### When to apply
- Product strategy engagements where the question is what to build
- Innovation roadmap work — use the four risks as a prioritization lens
- Organizational design for product teams

## 8. EATING THE BIG FISH — Adam Morgan

### The Challenger Brand
A challenger brand is not defined by size. It is defined by state of mind: a brand that has ambitions disproportionate to its conventional resources and must behave differently to achieve them. The challenger reframes the rules of the category rather than playing by them.

### Lighthouse Identity
The foundation of challenger brand strategy. A lighthouse identity is:
- **Strong enough** to attract some and repel others
- **Oriented** — it knows exactly where it stands
- **Self-referential** — it judges the world by its own standards rather than by the category's

A lighthouse brand doesn't try to appeal to everyone. It stakes out a position clear enough that people who share its values find it, and people who don't recognize it's not for them.

### The Eight Credos of the Challenger
Morgan's framework for how challengers behave:
1. **Intelligent Naivety** — Approach the category with fresh eyes; ask why it works the way it does
2. **Build a Lighthouse Identity** — Create a clear point of view about the world
3. **Assume Thought Leadership of the Category** — Own the conversation about where the category is going
4. **Create Symbols of Re-evaluation** — Single, striking acts that force customers to reconsider assumptions
5. **Sacrifice** — Deliberately limit what you offer to amplify what you stand for
6. **Overcommit** — Commit resources disproportionately to the most important expressions of identity
7. **Use Advertising and Publicity as a Vehicle for Challenger Strategy** — Communication is part of the strategic act, not a separate function
8. **Become Idea-Centered, Not Consumer-Centered** — Lead with what you believe, not what research says customers want

### Thought Leadership of the Category
The challenger that owns the conversation about where the category is going becomes the reference point for the whole category — including the leader. This is disproportionate leverage: you set the frame everyone else has to respond to.

### When to apply
- Brand strategy for non-market-leaders
- Repositioning work where the client needs to shift the competitive conversation
- New business pitches for challenger clients

### Where it breaks down
Challenger strategy requires genuine conviction and willingness to repel. Organizations that want to challenge without sacrificing — that want the Lighthouse Identity but also to appeal broadly — undermine the model. Intelligent naivety also has limits: there are things that work the way they do for reasons that survive scrutiny.

## Cross-Framework Connections

These frameworks are not independent. They connect and reinforce each other:

- **Rumelt's Diagnosis** + **Kahneman's System 2** — Rigorous diagnosis is a deliberate override of System 1's rush to conclusion
- **Dunford's Positioning** + **JTBD** — Competitive alternatives and unique attributes are best defined through the jobs lens: what job are customers hiring your product to do, and what are the alternatives they'd hire instead?
- **Sharp's Mental Availability** + **Morgan's Challenger** — Challengers build mental availability through Symbols of Re-evaluation and Lighthouse Identity rather than through repetition and reach — different mechanisms, same cognitive goal
- **Lafley's Where to Play** + **Ulwick's Opportunity Algorithm** — Where to play is best decided by where importance is high and satisfaction is low — the opportunity algorithm provides the analytical substrate for the cascade choice
- **Cagan's Discovery** + **Rumelt's Kernel** — Product discovery is the operational practice that produces the Diagnosis step; the guiding policy and coherent actions are the output of discovery, not its input
